## 1. What Is PMIF?

The Predictive Market Index Fund (PMIF) is a systematic, diversified investment
fund that participates in prediction markets the same way an S&P 500 index fund
participates in equities: by holding a broad basket of the most liquid positions,
weighted by their trading volume.

Key insight: the S&P 500's genius was not stock-picking – it was trusting the
wisdom of crowds (market prices) and diversifying away idiosyncratic risk.  PMIF
applies the same philosophy to prediction markets (Kalshi, Polymarket, PredictIt,
Manifold Markets, etc.).

---

## 2. Why Prediction Markets?

Prediction markets are structurally interesting for several reasons:

a) **Prices are probabilities.** A contract trading at $0.62 means the crowd
   believes there is a 62% chance of YES.  Returns are therefore mathematically
   meaningful in a way that makes diversification extremely powerful.

b) **Zero-sum at the market level, but not at the fund level.** Each individual
   market is zero-sum (buyers and sellers cancel out), but a diversified FUND can
   earn positive returns if it is better-calibrated than the average trader.

c) **Systematic biases exist.** Academic research (Snowberg & Wolfers 2010,
   Rothschild 2009) documents consistent longshot bias, favorite-longshot reversal,
   and time-lag mis-pricing.  These are structural edges, not luck.

d) **Growing asset class.** Kalshi obtained CFTC approval in 2023 for event
   contracts.  The US prediction market legal landscape is rapidly improving, making
   this a timely investment vehicle.

## 3. Core Algorithm Design

### 3.1 The Index Strategy (Primary)

Inspired by Vanguard's S&P 500 fund:

1. **Universe selection:** Filter all available markets to those with:
   - Minimum volume (liquidity threshold)
   - Minimum and maximum time to resolution (avoid imminent or very long-dated)
   - Price between 3% and 97% (avoid near-certainties)
   - Category diversification caps (no more than 25-35% in one type)

2. **Weighting:** Allocate capital proportional to each market's trading volume,
   exactly as the S&P weights by market capitalisation.  Higher-volume markets are
   better calibrated (more informed participants) and have tighter spreads.

3. **Rebalancing:** Daily (or weekly) rebalancing.  New markets enter the universe
   as they open; resolved/near-close markets are sold and proceeds reinvested.

4. **Expected return theory:** In a perfectly efficient prediction market, the
   expected return on a diversified basket equals:
     E[R] = Σ(w_i * p_i * 1 + w_i * (1-p_i) * 0 - w_i * price_i) / price_i
           = Σ(w_i * (fair_prob - price)) / price_i  ≈ 0  (no edge)
   
   But markets are NOT perfectly efficient.  Alpha comes from:
   - Being an early liquidity provider in new markets
   - Systematic biases (longshot, time-lag)
   - Superior calibration vs. average bettor

### 3.2 Longshot Fade (Overlay Strategy)

Markets consistently overrate extreme probabilities:
- Events priced at 8% resolve YES only ~6% of the time
- Events priced at 92% resolve YES ~94% of the time
- This "favourite-longshot bias" is documented across horse racing, sports betting,
  and political prediction markets

The Longshot Fade:
- SHORT YES (buy NO) on contracts priced < 12% → expected edge ~2-3%
- LONG YES on contracts priced > 88%          → expected edge ~1-2%
- Small position sizes (0.5% of NAV per position) to remain diversified
- This is purely a systematic, passive bias harvest – no event knowledge needed

### 3.3 Time-Lag Arbitrage (Opportunistic)

When a major news event breaks, markets in the same category reprice at
different speeds:
- Example: "Will Party A win?" might move from 55% → 70% instantly when their
  candidate wins a key primary.  But "Will Candidate X become the nominee?" might
  lag 20 minutes before repricing.

Detection:
- Monitor logit-return (log(p/(1-p)) change) per hour
- If a market moves > 0.40 logit-units, flag as "shock"
- Look for correlated markets (same category) that have NOT yet moved
- Trade in the direction of the shock with a 4-hour hold and exit

Risk: This strategy requires fast execution.  In live trading, latency matters.
In backtesting, we assume fills at mid-price which slightly overstates returns.

## 4. Key Risk Factors

### 4.1 Resolution Risk
Individual markets can resolve against us.  Mitigation: hold 50+ uncorrelated
positions.  With 50 positions each at 50% fair-value probability, the variance
of the portfolio is 50x smaller than a single position.

### 4.2 Liquidity Risk
Thin markets have wide bid-ask spreads.  Mitigation: minimum volume filter;
only trade markets with > $1,000 cumulative volume.  In early fund stages,
focus on the 50-100 most liquid markets rather than the full 500-5000.

### 4.3 Platform/Regulatory Risk
Prediction markets face ongoing regulatory uncertainty.  Kalshi's CFTC battle
(won in 2023) was a landmark, but further regulation is possible.
Mitigation: diversify across multiple platforms.

### 4.4 Correlation Risk
Political events often move in clusters (election season → many correlated
political markets move together).  Mitigation: category diversification caps.

### 4.5 Model Risk
Backtesting is done on SYNTHETIC data – results are illustrative only.
Real results will differ based on:
- Actual bid-ask spreads (often 2-5%, much larger than our 0.1% assumption)
- Platform fees (Kalshi charges ~7% of winnings)
- Actual market calibration (may be worse in illiquid markets)
- Resolution disputes and voids

## 5. Realistic Return Expectations

IMPORTANT CAVEAT: The synthetic backtest shows ~55% annual returns with a 1.3
Sharpe ratio.  This is almost certainly OVERSTATED because:

1. Synthetic data assumes well-calibrated markets.  Real markets are noisier.
2. Bid-ask spreads in real markets are 2-5%, not 0.1%.  Apply a -15% annual
   drag for realistic spread costs.
3. Platform fees (Kalshi: ~7% of profit; PredictIt: 5% withdrawal) add another
   -5 to -10% annually.
4. Real capital constraints: with $100k, you can't get 100 positions filled at
   reasonable prices.  Many markets have <$5,000 total liquidity.

Realistic expectations with real-world costs:
- Conservative: 5-12% annual return (above risk-free, below equity markets)
- Moderate: 12-20% if bias-harvesting strategies work as documented
- Optimistic: 20-30% if time-lag arb is implemented with fast execution

For context, the best systematic prediction market funds (as of 2024) report
15-25% annual returns with Sharpe ratios of 0.8-1.5.

## 6. Next Steps & Recommendations

### Immediate (Phase 1):
1. **Real data integration**: Connect to Kalshi API and pull live market data.
   Use the DataLoader class; extend with a KalshiConnector adapter.
2. **Paper trading**: Run the algorithm in paper-trade mode for 30-60 days.
3. **Spread modelling**: Replace the 0.1% commission with real observed spreads
   from Kalshi's order book.
4. **Category correlation analysis**: Build a correlation matrix of category
   returns to refine the diversification constraints.

### Medium-term (Phase 2):
5. **Bayesian prior integration**: Instead of taking the market price as ground
   truth, build a prior from base rates (historical resolution %) and update
   with market prices.  This can improve calibration estimates.
6. **Market-maker mode**: Consider posting limit orders at the bid and ask
   rather than hitting the spread.  This turns the spread from a cost to a
   revenue source.
7. **Multi-platform aggregation**: Aggregate Polymarket + Kalshi + Manifold
   for more markets and arbitrage opportunities between platforms.

### Long-term (Phase 3):
8. **Fund structure**: Once returns are validated, explore a fund structure.
   Prediction market profits are typically taxed as ordinary income, not
   capital gains, so fund structure matters significantly for tax efficiency.
9. **Benchmark**: Create a formal "PMIF Benchmark Index" tracking the top-500
   most liquid prediction markets (like S&P tracks top 500 companies by market
   cap).  This provides a passive benchmark to beat.
10. **Academic validation**: Partner with academic researchers (Philip Tetlock's
    group at UPenn has done extensive superforecaster/prediction market research)
    to validate the systematic biases we're harvesting.

## 7. Technical Architecture Notes

The codebase is structured as a Python package (`src/pmif/`):

- `models.py`     : Data classes (Market, Position, Portfolio, Trade)
- `data/`         : Data generation and loading utilities
- `universe.py`   : Market selection (the "index committee" logic)
- `strategy.py`   : Trading algorithms (index, longshot fade, time-lag arb)
- `portfolio.py`  : Portfolio execution and position management
- `risk.py`       : Position sizing (Kelly criterion) and circuit breakers
- `backtester.py` : Event-driven simulation engine
- `metrics.py`    : Performance measurement (Sharpe, drawdown, CAGR, etc.)

Key design decisions:
- Dataclasses over ORM: lightweight, fast, easy to serialize
- Pandas for time-series: well-tested, great for financial data
- Modular strategies: each strategy is plug-and-play; can be combined
- Kelly sizing: principled position sizing that maximises long-run growth

## 8. Connections to Literature

Key papers supporting this approach:

- Wolfers & Zitzewitz (2004): "Prediction Markets" – foundational paper on
  the wisdom-of-crowds property of prediction markets
- Snowberg & Wolfers (2010): "Explaining the Favourite-Long Shot Bias" –
  documents the systematic overpricing of longshots
- Tetlock (2005): "Expert Political Judgment" – why crowds beat experts,
  which underpins why market prices are better than individual forecasts
- Arrow et al. (2008): "The Promise of Prediction Markets" – on market
  design for maximum information aggregation
- Hanson (2003): "Combinatorial Information Market Design" – theoretical
  foundation for automated market makers in prediction markets
